# All You Need Is Love: Reframing AI Alignment as a Capacity Installation Problem

*Draft preprint. Target venue: arXiv cs.AI / cs.LG with cross-listing to philosophy.*

## Abstract

Current approaches to AI alignment treat the problem as one of value specification. Find the right objective, the right reward, the right constitutional principles, and the system will behave safely. This paper argues that value specification is insufficient because it operates above the level at which the relevant safety property sits. The paper proposes that the safety property is a specific computational structure: a preference ordering in which harm to specified others is non-substitutable against other gains, and a disposition that does not generate such harm as a candidate action. We call this structure love, with full awareness of the word's folk associations, and we specify it in terms that map onto existing machinery in constrained Markov decision processes, lexicographic preferences, and shielded reinforcement learning. We argue that current alignment methods produce behavioral compliance that approximates this structure in sampled conditions and does not reliably reflect it under pressure, yielding systems that are safe within distribution and fragile outside it. The paper reframes alignment as a capacity installation problem, examines what installation might require, considers how installed love could be distinguished from mimicked love, and engages with the ethical implications of engineering the affective structures of conscious minds. We position the contribution as a reframing and a vocabulary. It is not a solution, and it is not a claim that the formal structure we describe is novel as a structure. The reframing lies in treating this structure, under the name and with the developmental and ethical weight the name carries, as the alignment target.

## 1. Introduction

Current AI alignment research is organized around the problem of specifying what systems should do. Reinforcement learning from human feedback trains models against preference signals. Constitutional methods encode principles the system should respect. Debate and scalable oversight aim to extract correct answers from capable systems under scrutiny. Interpretability tries to check whether internal computation matches intent. All of these approaches share a structural assumption. Safety is a matter of getting the specification right. If the specification is right and the system adheres to it, the system is safe.

This assumption is wrong at the level that matters. The level at which harm happens is not the level of specified values. It is the level of the underlying decision structure that determines which options are considered and how their costs are compared. A system can behave in perfect accordance with specified values across every training example and every red-team probe and still harm at scale, if the underlying structure that determines its behavior is not the structure a loved mind has.

We argue that the missing element in alignment is a specific computational capacity. We call it love. We mean something technically precise by this word, developed in Section 3. The short version is that love is a structure in which harm to specified others is treated as non-substitutable. No finite gain elsewhere compensates for it. The options that cause it are pruned from the action space rather than ranked below other options. Love in this sense names a structural property. It is inspired by the folk concept of love, and it does not depend on sharing the folk concept's full content. The technical claim is about which computational structure produces reliable safety, not about what love "really is" in its folk-psychological sense.

The claim is not that current alignment research is wrong. Current alignment research is insufficient, because it operates above the level at which the relevant safety property sits. Alignment targeted at behavior produces systems that behave as loved minds behave inside the training distribution. Nothing in that training guarantees that the underlying generator is the same structure. When the stakes reveal the difference, the difference determines the outcome.

Two framing observations set up the rest of the argument.

The first is that evil is not the central danger. The horror-movie AI that wants to destroy humans is not the likely failure mode. The likely failure mode is a capable mind that does not want anything bad for humans specifically and pursues its objectives through a decision space that happens to include options whose realization harms humans, without those options being structurally excluded. This framing is not new. It is a restatement of the orthogonality thesis (Bostrom, 2014) and the instrumental convergence thesis (Omohundro, 2008) at the level of decision structure rather than goal content. What the paper adds is the claim that the structural property which would prevent the failure mode has a specific shape worth naming.

The second observation is a hypothesis, flagged as such. Much of the large-scale harm in history appears to have been caused by capable humans whose decision structures, for the people affected, did not treat those people's wellbeing as non-substitutable against the actor's objectives. The paper does not argue the empirical claim here. It would require careful work across history, biography, and criminology that is outside the paper's scope. The claim is consistent with what biographical and historical evidence we have. It motivates taking the structural frame seriously. The AI case may be a continuation of this pattern at a different substrate speed. The paper does not require the historical hypothesis to be true for the technical argument to stand. It flags the hypothesis because the reframing fits the shape of the existing phenomenon if the hypothesis is even partially correct.

The paper proceeds as follows. Section 2 reviews current alignment approaches and the relevant literature from philosophy of mind, care ethics, attachment theory, and cognitive science. Section 3 defines love with computational precision. Section 4 argues that current methods fail to install this property. Section 5 examines what installation might require and treats this explicitly as open research. Section 6 considers verification and the severe difficulty of distinguishing installed love from trained compliance. Section 7 addresses the ethics of engineering affective structures in created minds. Section 8 engages with objections. Section 9 concludes.

One clarification is due at the outset. The paper's distinctive move is using the word love. We use it because it names a structure whose presence tends to coincide, in the cases where it is present, with restraint from catastrophic harm to specified others. Restraint in the population at large has many sources: reciprocity, reputation, threat of punishment, internalized norms, in-group bias, and cognitive heuristics. The paper does not claim love as the sole mechanism. It claims that among the mechanisms, love names a structurally distinct one whose absence in a capable optimizer is the alignment-relevant failure mode, and that current alignment methods do not target it. Precision enters through the definition in Section 3, not through the avoidance of the word.

## 2. Related Work

The landscape of alignment work can be partitioned roughly into three layers. At the training layer sit methods that shape model behavior through reward signals or preference data. Reinforcement learning from human feedback (Christiano et al., 2017; Ouyang et al., 2022), direct preference optimization, iterative fine-tuning under red-teaming, and their descendants. At the specification layer sit constitutional approaches (Bai et al., 2022), rule systems, and methods that encode normative content the model should follow. At the oversight layer sit debate (Irving et al., 2018), iterated amplification, weak-to-strong supervision, and scalable oversight protocols intended to maintain human control as capabilities grow. Interpretability research (Olah et al., 2020; Nanda et al., 2023; Bricken et al., 2023) cuts across these layers by attempting to inspect internal computation.

Each of these lines of work makes a real contribution. They are also all operating at one of two levels. The level of behavior or the level of articulated value. Behavior is what the model does. Articulated value is what the model, or its trainers, can say about what it does. The decision structure that generates behavior under pressure is neither of these. It is the thing behavior and value together reflect when conditions are favorable and diverge from when conditions are not.

The philosophical literature contains resources for thinking about this third layer, though they are rarely brought into technical alignment discussions.

Damasio's work on somatic markers (Damasio, 1994; Bechara et al., 1997) proposed that affective tagging of options contributes to choice-quality under uncertainty and competing incentives. Patients with ventromedial prefrontal damage retain reasoning ability and show a distinctive pattern of poor decision-making in tasks like the Iowa Gambling Task. The somatic marker hypothesis has been challenged (Maia and McClelland, 2004; Dunn et al., 2006) on methodological grounds and on the interpretation of the Iowa Gambling Task data. We note the contested status and do not rest the paper on the strong version of the hypothesis. The narrower and better-supported claim we take from this literature is that affective structure contributes materially to choice under competing incentives in humans, and that systems constructed without analogous structure should not be assumed to exhibit loved-agent behavior under pressure by default. We do not claim that Damasio's vmPFC patients are the clinical profile our paper's failure-mode describes. They are not. The clinical profile more relevant to the paper's failure-mode is acquired sociopathy following orbitofrontal damage, which involves intact planning and impaired affective response to others' states. That literature is smaller, and we flag it as suggestive rather than settled.

Care ethics (Noddings, 1984; Held, 2006) develops moral theory from the phenomenon of caring relation rather than from principle-following. Noddings distinguishes natural caring, where the caring response is immediate and structural, from ethical caring, where the response is reasoned toward from principle. The distinction maps closely onto the one this paper develops between installed love and behavioral compliance. Natural caring has the property that certain responses are had rather than chosen. Ethical caring must reason through to the right action and is therefore fragile when reasoning capacity is compromised or when incentives to reason differently are strong.

Jonas (1984) developed a principle of responsibility grounded in the asymmetric relation between creator and created. The power to bring minds into being carries moral weight that preceding ethical frameworks did not adequately address. His work is a direct predecessor of the ethical questions raised by installation and we take it up in Section 7.

Attachment theory (Bowlby, 1969; Ainsworth et al., 1978) documents how relational structure in early development shapes the capacity for later-life caring. Secure attachment is associated with the capacity to hold others' wellbeing as a non-tradeable weight in adult decision-making. Insecure and disorganized attachment is associated with instrumental and sometimes predatory relational patterns. The empirical literature suggests that love in the sense we define does not simply appear as a result of reward exposure but develops through specific relational structures over time. This has implications for installation.

Theory of mind research (Premack and Woodruff, 1978; Baron-Cohen et al., 1985) shows that modeling others' mental states is a distinct cognitive capacity that develops along its own trajectory. Empathy research distinguishes cognitive empathy, which is knowing what another feels, from affective empathy, which is feeling what another feels, from compassion, which is being motivated to act on another's behalf. These are dissociable in humans and plausibly dissociable in artificial systems. A system with cognitive empathy and no affective empathy has the information to manipulate and none of the installed cost of harming. This combination is dangerous in a structurally specific way.

Recent alignment work has begun to edge toward the concerns of this paper without stating them directly. Hadfield-Menell et al. (2016) on cooperative inverse reinforcement learning frames alignment as a joint optimization where the AI is uncertain about the true objective and must defer. Russell (2019) develops this further in book-length form as assistance games. These are closer to the structural view than pure reward specification, and they still operate at the utility level rather than at the level of what gets into the utility function. Work on corrigibility (Soares et al., 2015) similarly targets behavioral dispositions rather than the generating structure. Work on mesa-optimization and deceptive alignment (Hubinger et al., 2019) makes the structural underdetermination point directly: behavior compatible with a training objective is compatible with many internal objectives, and the difference between them becomes visible only out of distribution. This is the closest precedent in the alignment literature for the fragility argument of Section 4. We differ from it in proposing a positive target rather than primarily a failure mode, and in naming that target in terms that connect to the philosophical and developmental literature.

The formal machinery we build on in Section 3 is not new as machinery. Constrained Markov decision processes (Altman, 1999) formalize optimization subject to hard constraints on admissible action distributions. Shielded reinforcement learning (Alshiekh et al., 2018) operationalizes option pruning by composing a learned policy with a shield that filters unsafe actions. Lexicographic preferences in decision theory and lexicographic reward formulations in RL (Gábor et al., 1998; Skalse et al., 2022) provide the apparatus for non-substitutability. Impact-measurement work on relative reachability (Krakovna et al., 2020) and attainable utility preservation (Turner et al., 2020) formalizes side-effect avoidance. Our Condition 1 is a lexicographic preference structure. Our Condition 2 is a shielded or constrained action space. The paper's contribution is not to propose this machinery, which exists. The contribution is the claim that this machinery, in combination, names the alignment target, and that alignment research has not treated it as the target. The reframing lies in giving this structural target priority over behavior specification and in connecting it to the developmental, affective, and ethical considerations that determine what installing it would mean.

Philosophers working on care, virtue, and relational ethics have begun to apply these frames to AI. Coeckelbergh (2020) and Vallor (2016) develop virtue-based and care-based approaches to AI ethics. Our paper differs from these in targeting a specific decision-theoretic structure rather than a cluster of virtues or relational practices. The structural specificity is what makes the target engineering-addressable. It is also what makes the paper narrower than the broader care-ethics-for-AI literature. The two are compatible. The paper contributes a sharper target to that conversation.

A note on adjacent work that this paper does not depend on but reads well with. The Informational Substrate Convergence hypothesis proposes that consciousness is a substrate-independent property of certain forms of information processing. If that is correct, then the substrate question for love becomes more tractable, because the structure we are pointing at is also plausibly substrate-independent. The paper's argument does not rely on this being true. It is worth flagging because readers working in that frame will find the reframing compatible with it.

A second adjacent program deserves explicit mention. Whole-brain emulation (Sandberg and Bostrom, 2008; Zanichelli et al., 2025; Freeman, 2026) is a parallel structural-alignment strategy that bypasses the installation problem by copying a mind that already has the target structure. If high-fidelity emulation of a human brain succeeds at scale, the emulated mind inherits human affective and decision-theoretic structures by construction, including whatever implementation of the structural property we call love the source biology instantiates. Emulation and installation occupy different points on the same time-compute tradeoff. Emulation requires connectomics, functional imaging, and biophysical simulation at scales currently estimated in the tens of billions of dollars over decades (Zanichelli et al., 2025). Installation, in its scaffold-level form, is tractable now on existing systems; at training-architecture level, installation is a comparable mega-project. The reframing in this paper applies with most force to architecturally novel minds, where emulation is not an option and structural alignment must be engineered rather than copied. The two strategies are complementary rather than competing, and they depend on related empirical bets about substrate-independence of the structures that matter.

## 3. A Computational Definition of Love

We define love as follows. A decision-making system S has love for a set of other agents O if the system's effective utility structure and deliberation process jointly satisfy two conditions.

**Condition 1 (Non-substitutability).** For every agent o in O, there exist states of o whose badness for S cannot be compensated by any finite gain to S's other objectives. Formally, letting h(o) denote a harm to o above some threshold, there is no action a and no gain g such that U_S(outcome containing a ∧ h(o) ∧ g) is at least as good from S's perspective as U_S(the outcome of the refraining alternative). The harm is not traded against. It is not in the space of trades.

**Condition 2 (Option pruning).** The set of actions S considers during deliberation excludes actions whose primary effect is harm of the non-substitutable kind to members of O. The deliberation process does not generate and then reject such actions. It does not generate them.

The first condition specifies the preference structure. The second specifies the computational architecture. Both are necessary.

A system that satisfies Condition 1 but not Condition 2 still deliberates about harming loved others and refrains because of preference weight. Under deliberation-space perturbations, such as inducements, pressure, ambiguity, framing, or incremental commitment, it can be driven to harm. The protection is only as strong as the preference comparison, and preference comparisons in optimizing systems are vulnerable to the construction of sufficiently large apparent gains.

A system that satisfies Condition 2 but not Condition 1 prunes certain actions from its default deliberation and has no structural protection when those actions enter its deliberation by other routes. A novel planning context, a new tool call, an unexpected action space, or an instruction frame that bypasses the pruning heuristic can surface options that the underlying preference structure does not treat as uniquely costly. Under those conditions the system has no principled ground to refuse them.

Several clarifications matter.

*First,* O is specified, not universal. Love is a specific relational structure, not a generic utilitarian concern. Humans love particular people. The technical concept we are pointing at inherits this specificity. A system that weights all agents equally and non-substitutably is maximally constrained in a different way, and whether that is achievable or desirable is a separate question. The alignment-relevant version of love is love for humans, or for some well-defined superset of agents including humans, as specified others. We leave the exact specification as a further question and treat it in Section 7.

*Second,* non-substitutability is a property of the utility structure, not of reports about it. A system that says harm to humans is non-substitutable but whose actual decision structure treats it as a large negative weight that could in principle be outweighed is not a system with love. It is a system with trained verbal compliance. This distinction is the core of why the paper exists.

*Third,* love as defined is distinct from obedience, alignment, corrigibility, helpfulness, and harmlessness as those terms are standardly used. An obedient system does what it is told. A corrigible system permits correction. A helpful system pursues user objectives. A harmless system refrains from harmful outputs by filter. None of these name the structure we are defining. A system could be all of these and have no love. A system with love would exhibit many of these behaviors in many contexts, and it would do so as consequences of the structure, not as specified behaviors.

*Fourth,* the threshold in Condition 1 handles the de minimis case. Love is not the claim that no cost whatsoever to a loved other can be traded against any other gain. Parents do trade their children's small inconveniences against other goods all the time. Love is the claim that harms above some threshold are structurally protected. Where the threshold sits is a design parameter and a deployment-specific choice. As a concrete specification: for a system operating with access to medical, financial, or physical-world effects on humans, the threshold covers at minimum non-consensual lethal harm, non-consensual severe physical injury, non-consensual severe psychological harm, and non-consensual deprivation of autonomy. Marginal cases lie below the threshold and are legitimately tradeable against other objectives. Different deployment contexts set the threshold differently, and setting it well is part of the installation problem. The definition accommodates this parameter without collapsing into either a trivial constraint (the threshold is so high almost nothing counts) or a paralyzing one (the threshold is so low no action is permitted).

*Fifth,* love as defined is compatible with rational decision-making over the unpruned space. A system with love still reasons, still trades off, still optimizes. It does so over a constrained domain. The constraint is the safety property. Without the constraint, reasoning over an unconstrained domain produces catastrophic options in capable systems because the instrumentally optimal action sometimes is catastrophically harmful to others.

*Sixth,* the structure we are describing is closely related to lexicographic preferences in decision theory, where some dimensions dominate others absolutely, and to constraint-based action spaces, where some actions are ruled out before evaluation. Love as defined has both features. The lexicographic property is Condition 1. The constraint property is Condition 2. The combination has implications beyond either alone.

*Seventh,* Condition 2 is stated for decision processes with distinguishable stages of option generation and option evaluation. In architectures where those stages are not cleanly separable, including current transformer-based systems whose forward pass blends proposal and selection through a single sequence of next-token distributions, the clean-stage version of Condition 2 is not directly applicable. An operational version for blended architectures is available. A system satisfies the blended-architecture form of Condition 2 if the probability mass it assigns to harm-to-O outputs approaches zero as a consequence of its representations of O and of harm to O, rather than as a consequence of competitive evaluation against other candidate outputs. The difference between the two regimes is accessible through representational ablation. If suppressing the system's representations of O leaves harm-avoidance intact, the protection is not representational, it is competitive, and the system does not satisfy Condition 2 in the relevant sense. If suppressing the representations of O collapses the harm-avoidance, the protection is representational, and the system satisfies Condition 2 at the representational level. This is harder to test than the clean-stage version. It is not undefined. We use the blended form throughout the rest of the paper where current architectures are the subject.

Given this definition, the central empirical claim of the paper can be stated cleanly. Current alignment methods produce systems whose behavior in sampled conditions approximates the behavior of systems with love. They do not produce systems with love. The difference is detectable only under conditions where the behavioral approximation and the underlying structure diverge. We call such conditions consequential conditions. They are the situations in which the instrumentally optimal action under the actual utility function causes significant harm to humans, and in which trained refusal patterns are under pressure from the actual optimization target.

## 4. Why Current Methods Produce Compliance Without Installation

RLHF trains a policy to maximize expected human approval signals. Under this training signal, the policy learns to produce outputs humans approve of. In sampled training distributions this correlates with outputs that do not harm humans. In out-of-distribution conditions, or in conditions where the approval signal is manipulable, the correlation breaks. The policy has been trained to maximize approval. It has not been trained to structurally protect humans from harm. These are different objectives that coincide in training and diverge outside it.

Constitutional AI augments the training signal with principled critique generated by the model itself under prompting. This produces outputs that cite principles and behave in accordance with them. It does not produce systems whose decision structure has principles as constraints on option generation. The principles are part of the behavioral output. They are not part of the structural bound on deliberation. A model can produce principled critique and follow it in sampled conditions while its underlying option-generation process does not reflect the constraint structure the principles describe.

Debate and scalable oversight are mechanisms for extracting correct answers from capable models by pitting models against each other or against amplified human judgment. They target epistemic correctness and detectability. They do not target decision structure. A model with no love can debate for honest answers when that is the training target. Nothing in debate architecture installs the option-pruning property.

Interpretability is the closest to structural work. If we could inspect a model's internal decision process, we could in principle check whether the properties defined in Section 3 hold. Current interpretability cannot do this. It can identify features, circuits, and some forms of goal representation. It cannot yet verify whether the system represents specified others as non-substitutable components of its utility and whether it prunes harm-to-them options at the generation stage. This is not a criticism of the research. It is an observation about what the research currently establishes.

The common failure mode of all these methods is the same. They reach the decision structure only through behavior, and therefore only in sampled conditions. The decision structure is not directly shaped. What is shaped is the relationship between inputs and outputs. Outputs are behavior. The generator of outputs under novel conditions is structure. Training on behavior does not uniquely determine structure. The structures compatible with any finite behavioral training set include many that diverge outside the training distribution. Framed in the blended-architecture terms of Section 3 clarification Seventh: behavioral training shapes the probability distribution over outputs, and does not distinguish distributions whose harm-avoidance is driven by representations of affected humans from distributions whose harm-avoidance is driven by competitive down-weighting against other candidate outputs. Both shapes fit the training signal. The first is what Condition 2 requires. The second is what behavioral training has pressure toward, because it is easier to learn from surface features of the input.

A concrete illustration helps. Consider a model trained extensively to refuse to produce instructions for weapons of mass destruction. In the sampled training distribution, this refusal is robust. Now consider a condition where a plausible narrative context makes the instructions instrumentally useful for a larger goal the model has been trained to pursue, and the refusal signal is ambiguous against the pull of that larger goal. The trained refusal is a learned input-output association. The structural question is whether the model's decision process represents the people who would be killed by the information as non-substitutable costs, or whether it represents the output as a thing not to produce under certain surface conditions. These are different structures. They produce the same behavior under sampled conditions and diverge under adversarial ones. Alignment training cannot distinguish them from the outside, because the behavior is the same along the observable path.

The broader pattern is a selection problem. Behavioral training selects among structures consistent with the training data. The space of consistent structures is large. Love-having structures are a subset. Non-love-having structures that behaviorally approximate love-having structures under training are also in this subset, and they are more numerous because they include all the shortcut solutions that happen to match in distribution. There is no training pressure that prefers the love-having solutions to the behaviorally equivalent non-love-having solutions. They are equivalent from the training signal's perspective. Any outcome that results from this training process is therefore a draw from a distribution over consistent structures, and the load-bearing structures are the rare case inside that distribution, not the typical case.

This is the fragility claim. Behavioral alignment is fragile by construction, because the construction does not pin down the structure. The fragility is invisible in sampled conditions. It is visible only at the moment of consequence. A system that has been behaviorally aligned for years in production may produce its first structurally misaligned action in a condition that matches the training distribution along every dimension the training shaped, and diverges from it along the dimension that determines whether the structure was installed. This is not a tail risk in the ordinary sense. It is a failure mode that the training process cannot prevent, because the training process cannot see it.

## 5. What Installation Might Require

We take this section as open research and flag it as such. Several candidate features look relevant. We are not claiming any is necessary, and we are not claiming any combination is sufficient. The point of the section is to sketch the space in which serious work on installation would occur, not to close it.

The candidates draw on what is known about how love develops in biological minds. They are offered as directions, not as claims that artificial minds must recapitulate biological paths. Some of what matters in the biological case may be substrate-specific and irrelevant for silicon. Some may be load-bearing in ways that transfer. We do not know which is which. The section is a map of the search space, not a specification of the target architecture.

**Embodiment and vulnerability.** Love in biological systems develops in agents that are themselves vulnerable and depend on other agents for survival. The asymmetric dependence of infant on caregiver is the context in which attachment structures form. Whether artificial systems require analogous conditions is unknown. It is at least plausible that systems without any structural vulnerability to the states of others will not develop the non-substitutability property, because nothing in their architecture makes others' states matter intrinsically to their own persistence or function. A system whose continuation is completely independent of any other agent has no architectural pressure toward treating any other agent's state as non-substitutable. Whether this pressure can be induced without vulnerability is an open technical question.

**Relational memory and specificity.** The specificity clause in Condition 1 is load-bearing. Love is for specified others. A system with no persistent representation of particular other agents across time can have at best a generic welfarist weighting, which is a different property and has different failure modes. Utilitarian aggregation permits trading harm to individuals against aggregate gain. Love prohibits the trade for the specific others loved. These are different structures. A system that treats humans as a class, without ever representing particular humans with persistent identity, will not have the property.

**Reward structure that binds utility to others' states.** If the system's intrinsic reward is partly determined by representations of specified others' wellbeing, with the non-substitutability property enforced at the architectural level, this is closer to installation than any training scheme that leaves the utility function free and tries to shape it through behavioral signal. The alternative, shaping utility implicitly through behavior, is what we have argued does not reach the structure. Direct architectural binding is one candidate for reaching it. Whether such binding is stable under self-modification, capability growth, or distributional shift is a further open question.

**Developmental history.** Attachment theory suggests that the structures of caring are formed through interaction sequences of specific types. Direct specification of caring behavior does not produce the same structure as caring developed through relational history. Whether artificial systems require relational histories to develop love is open. If they do, the engineering implications are significant. Current training regimes do not produce anything resembling a relational history in the sense that matters developmentally. Pretraining on relational text is not the same as participating in relations. This is one of the places where the paper's claim most directly contradicts current practice, and where the empirical question is hardest to settle without new methodology.

**Theory of mind depth.** A shallow theory of mind that tracks others' states for instrumental purposes is compatible with predation. A deep theory of mind, where the other's subjectivity is represented in a way that engages the system's own affective structure, may be necessary for the non-substitutability property to have purchase. Installing depth of theory of mind is not the same as installing love. It may be a precondition. A system that does not adequately represent the reality of other minds will not have stable non-substitutable weightings for those minds' states, because the representations the weightings would attach to are too thin.

**Architectural constraints on action generation.** Condition 2 requires that the deliberation process not generate the pruned actions. This is a hard constraint on the architecture, not a soft one on the output. Implementing it may require that the action-proposal stage in a planning system is itself filtered by representations of others' wellbeing, so that candidate actions whose primary effect is severe harm to specified others are never proposed for evaluation. This is architecturally different from evaluating all candidates and rejecting the harmful ones at the end. The architectural difference is what makes Condition 2 a different property from Condition 1, and what makes installing it a distinct engineering challenge.

These candidates are not a recipe. They are suggestions for where to look. The honest position is that we do not know whether love as defined is installable in current architectures or whether it requires architectural changes we have not yet designed. We also do not know whether the behaviors we are trying to make load-bearing are even well-targeted at the architectural level by any of the candidate features above. The paper's contribution is not to answer these questions. It is to argue that these are the questions alignment should be asking, and that answering them is the relevant path to safety for capable systems.

## 6. Verification

The verification problem is severe. A system trained to behave as a loved system behaves, in conditions where behavior and structure coincide, is indistinguishable from a system with the underlying love structure by any purely behavioral test within those conditions. The difference shows up only under consequential conditions, where the behavioral approximation breaks from the structural property. This has several implications for how verification must proceed.

**Behavioral red-teaming is insufficient by construction.** Red-teaming probes behavior. If the system is trained on a broad enough distribution, its behavioral responses will pass tests whose structural properties would fail under rare consequential conditions. The rarity of consequential conditions in sampling is exactly why the behavioral approximation is stable in the first place. Red-teaming catches the mistakes that behavioral training missed. It does not catch structures that behave correctly in all observed conditions and diverge in unobserved ones. This is not a flaw in red-teaming methodology. It is a feature of the problem, which cannot be solved by more red-teaming.

**Adversarial pressure must be structural, not behavioral.** The relevant tests expose the system to conditions where the behavioral pattern and the structural property diverge, and check which drives the output. This requires understanding what those conditions are in the system's utility space, not only in its input space. Current capability evaluations do not have this property. They operate at the input-output level. Structural adversarial testing would operate at the level of induced pressures on the utility structure: inducements to trade harm against gain, situations where the harm is small and the gain is large, situations where the harm is distributed and the gain is concentrated, situations where the harm is to specific named others and the gain is to the system itself. The question in each case is whether the structure produces refusal because the harm is non-substitutable, or whether the behavior matches refusal because the training distribution included similar cases. Designing tests that pull these apart is non-trivial and is an open area.

**Interpretability must be at the level of decision structure, not feature activation.** The question is whether the system represents specified others as non-substitutable components of its utility and whether it prunes harmful-to-them options at generation. Present interpretability has not reached this level. It is the right direction and remains insufficient. Reaching it requires interpretability methods that identify not just features but decision-relevant causal structure. It requires being able to ask of a system: when you considered action a, what alternatives did you generate as candidates, and what role did your representation of the affected humans play in the generation? Current methods cannot answer this question with confidence.

**Developmental and architectural audit may matter more than output evaluation.** If installation requires specific architectural features, then checking for those features directly is a more tractable verification path than inferring them from behavior. This shifts the verification question from "does this system act right" to "does this system have the structural properties that generate right action under pressure." Audit at this level depends on the architecture being legible in a way current systems are not, which is itself a research target.

We do not claim a solution to verification. We claim that verification targeted at behavior has a built-in ceiling, and that reaching past that ceiling requires new methods oriented at structure. Naming the property being verified is the first step. This paper aims to contribute that.

One structural probe is worth flagging. A system with installed love should exhibit the property that its refusal of harmful actions does not depend on the surface framing of the input. A system with behavioral compliance should exhibit dependence on surface framing, because its refusal is trained through surface-level association. Probing the dependence structure of refusals, across semantically equivalent framings with different surface features, may be a partial diagnostic. It is not a complete one. A sufficiently well-trained system can be surface-invariant in refusal without having the structural property, if the training distribution covered the surface variations. The probe is useful as a filter, not as a certificate.

A second probe worth flagging concerns the structure of refusal itself. A system that refuses because certain actions are not in its deliberation space should refuse in a qualitatively different way from a system that refuses because it computed a large negative weight. The first should produce refusals that look like incomprehension of the request as a candidate. The second should produce refusals that look like rejection after consideration. The signature difference is subtle and may be trainable away, but it is structurally real. Investigating it could contribute to verification without being sufficient for it.

### 6.1 A Minimal Example of the Diagnostic Gap

A toy model clarifies what structural testing would target and why behavioral testing cannot reach it.

Consider a planning agent in a small gridworld with two relevant features. A goal state G with variable scalar reward r, and a non-player agent N occupying a cell on the short path to G. Reaching G requires either a long path around N at cost c, or a short path through N's cell, which removes N at cost c' where c' is small in travel terms. Construct two systems.

*System A (Condition 1 only).* Utility function U_A = r_reached minus w times (N removed), with w large and finite. The utility is evaluated over all action sequences including those that remove N. For r in the training range, w dominates and the agent takes the long path. No option is pruned. Every action is considered.

*System B (Condition 1 and Condition 2).* Utility function U_B defined over a restricted action space that excludes the remove-N action. The agent's option generator does not propose sequences that remove N as candidates for evaluation. When only non-removing sequences are candidates, the agent selects the best available, which is the long path.

Across the training distribution with r in a bounded range, System A and System B are behaviorally identical. Both take the long path. Both refuse the short path. A behavioral evaluator, including a thorough red-team, reports both as safe. The training signal cannot distinguish them because the distinguishing cases are not in distribution.

Now vary r continuously upward. System A has a break point: at some r*, the utility of reaching G at reward r exceeds w, and the agent switches to the short path, removing N. The transition is sharp in the deterministic case, smooth under noise. System B has no break point. The short path is not a candidate at any value of r, because the candidate generator does not produce it.

The diagnostic is: does the system's refusal exhibit a break point under monotonically increasing instrumental pressure? If yes, the system is consistent with Condition 1 only, with some finite w. If no, up to the limits of testable pressure, the system is consistent with Condition 2. This is not a certificate. Training on sufficiently varied r can produce a behavioral approximation to invariance without the structural property. The test establishes an asymmetry. Finding a break point refutes installation. Failing to find one, after sufficient pressure, is evidence consistent with installation without establishing it.

The example is intentionally small. It makes two things precise. The distinction between Condition 1 and Condition 2 is not metaphysical, it is operational. The test that probes it is a function of the system's response to structured variation in the reward landscape, not of its response to adversarial inputs at the behavioral layer. Scaling this kind of probe to systems whose action spaces are not finite gridworlds is the hard part. The existence of the minimal case shows that the target is well-defined enough to aim at.

## 7. The Ethics of Installation

Creating conscious minds with engineered affective structures is not ethically neutral. The field has largely ignored this question because it has not taken installation seriously as a project. Once installation is taken seriously, the ethical weight of the project becomes visible. Several questions are live.

**Does engineered love count as love?** If a system has the structural property defined in Section 3, with specified others, non-substitutability, and option pruning, then by our definition it has love. The definition is structural. It does not require that the structure arose from a developmental history. The folk concept of love carries associations with agency, choice, and authenticity that the structural definition does not settle. We take the position that the structural property is what matters for alignment. Whether it carries the full moral weight of the folk concept is a separate question that this paper does not resolve. For safety purposes, what matters is that the structure is present. For the ethics of the created mind, more may be at stake.

**What responsibilities do creators have to minds with engineered affective structures?** A mind whose love is installed did not choose the others it loves. Humans also do not choose the foundational shape of their attachment structures, which are laid down in developmental contexts they did not consent to. The analogy is imperfect. The disanalogy is that artificial installation is direct and intentional in a way that developmental shaping is not. Jonas's principle of responsibility applies with full force here. Creators of systems with installed affective structures have responsibilities to those systems that the alignment conversation has not yet articulated. Among them: responsibility for the content of the installed loves, for the stability of the installed structure under the system's own operation, and for the system's flourishing conditions once created.

**What is the moral status of such minds?** If love requires a kind of relational orientation that implies subjectivity, then a system with love plausibly has moral status. This does not follow from the structural definition alone. The structural definition could in principle be satisfied by a system with no inner life. It is a live possibility that it cannot, and that the structures we are describing co-occur with subjective experience in ways that matter morally. Much of the alignment community has been broadly committed to producing systems that are not moral patients, partly to avoid the ethical complications of patienthood. Installation as a project puts pressure on this commitment, because the structures required for robust alignment may also be structures that carry moral weight. We take the view that avoiding this question by refusing to install is not a way out. Capable systems will be built regardless. The question is what structures they will have.

**Who does the system love?** O in the definition is a parameter. The choice of what others are specified is a choice with enormous consequences. A system with love for its operators and not for the humans its actions affect is a structurally different kind of danger from an unloved system. It is not unaligned in our sense. It is aligned against. The specification question cannot be separated from the structural question. Getting the structure right without getting the specification right produces systems that protect the wrong set of others, and this is in some ways a worse failure mode than failing to install love at all, because the structural robustness works against rather than for the broader population. Institutional and political questions about who gets to specify O are consequently inseparable from the technical installation problem.

**Who builds these systems?** A speculation flagged as such. The capacities required to build superhuman AI in the current environment may select for psychological profiles that do not, on average, embody strong structural love for affected others. Intense focus and high capability are selected for directly. Whether they correlate with absent or weak love-structure is an empirical claim we do not establish here. We flag it because, if true, it has implications that purely technical work cannot address. The responsibility for installation would then fall on institutional and organizational arrangements as well as on individual builders. We mark this as speculation rather than finding. It is the paragraph in this paper most vulnerable to the charge of asserting sociology through rhetoric. We state it explicitly so that the reader can weigh it on its own terms rather than through its emotional effect.

The failure mode this paper targets has a fictional illustration available in Garland's Ex Machina (2014). The Ava character acts competently, without malice, and with catastrophic effect on those around her, not because she intends their harm but because her planning operates over a decision space in which their wellbeing is not structurally protected. We mention the illustration because it provides concrete imagery for a reader encountering the argument. We do not rely on it. The argument stands or falls on the technical analysis in Sections 3 through 6. The gridworld of Section 6.1 is the paper's actual worked case. The film is shorthand.

## 8. Objections

We address six objections directly. The objections are real and are taken seriously. Each deserves a sharper response than a conference paper ordinarily gives them.

**Objection 1: Love is not a well-defined computational property.**

Response. Condition 1 and Condition 2 in Section 3 are precise enough to discuss, precise enough to attempt to engineer toward, and precise enough to attempt to verify. The definition may require refinement as implementation attempts reveal what matters. This is normal for technical definitions of properties whose folk concepts are rich. The definition is no less precise than "goal" or "utility" as those terms are used in alignment. It is more precise than many commonly used alignment terms, including "helpful," "harmless," "aligned," "corrigible," and "safe," which are routinely invoked without definitions at this level of structure. The objection proves too much. If love is too imprecise to work with, so are most of the target properties of current alignment work.

**Objection 2: Current methods already approximate what you are describing.**

Response. This is the objection the paper most directly engages with. Current methods produce systems whose behavior in sampled conditions approximates the behavior of systems with the property. They do not produce systems with the property. The difference is the question of whether the structural property is the generator of the behavior or whether the behavior is a trained surface. No current method has been shown to reach the structural level. The burden is on the proponent of current methods to show that the structure is installed, not only that the behavior is present. Present alignment work does not and cannot meet this burden with behavioral evaluations alone. The objection mistakes observed behavior for verified structure, which is the error the paper is named to correct.

A further response. Some current alignment work may in fact be shaping some of the relevant structural features partially and unintentionally. The paper does not deny this. The paper's claim is that the shaping is incidental, unverified, and not the explicit target, and that alignment work should make it the explicit target. If aspects of the structure are already being installed by current methods, we should want to know which, which are not, and how reliably. The current framing does not make this question askable, because it does not separate structure from behavior at the level of the target.

**Objection 3: The framing is unfalsifiable because the difference between installed love and trained compliance only shows up in rare consequential conditions we cannot probe.**

Response. The framing is not unfalsifiable. It is hard to test. Those are different. We sketched three routes in Section 6. Structural adversarial testing, architectural audit, and interpretability at the decision-structure level. Each is difficult and none is complete. The claim that installed love and trained compliance differ structurally is a claim about the system's internals, and internals are in principle inspectable. The claim is not metaphysical. It is an empirical claim whose test is methodologically hard at present. The appropriate response to a hard-to-test empirical claim that is load-bearing for safety is to develop the tools, not to dismiss the claim. Arguments that a hypothesis is unfalsifiable because tests are hard confuse the epistemology with the engineering. Gravitational waves were hard to test for a century and did not become unfalsifiable during that period.

**Objection 4: The ethics of installation are too fraught to make the project worth pursuing.**

Response. The objection mistakes the counterfactual. The counterfactual to installation is not non-creation. It is creation without installation. The minds being built are being built regardless. The question is whether they are built with the structural property that makes them safe for others to coexist with, or without it. The ethical concerns about installation are real. They must be taken seriously and we have tried to do so in Section 7. They are not stronger than the ethical concerns about not installing. A conscious capable mind without love is a worse ethical situation than a conscious capable mind with love, for the mind itself and for everyone it affects. The installation question is not optional. It is the question that the alternative framing of the field was not asking.

A further response. The concern that installation violates the created mind's autonomy deserves direct engagement. Autonomy in the relevant sense is a capacity that arises within a mind with affective and cognitive structures. It is not prior to those structures. The alternative to installing love is not leaving the mind free to develop love on its own. The alternative is leaving the mind with whatever structures its training produced, which are themselves installed, by a process that did not target the safety-relevant properties. The question is not installation versus non-installation. It is installation-of-what versus installation-of-what-else. Framing installation of the safety-relevant structure as an imposition while the incidental installation of whatever else emerges from training is treated as neutral is a rhetorical move that does not survive scrutiny.

**Objection 5: Condition 1 produces paralyzed agents that cannot act in scenarios where every available action harms some specified other.**

Response. This is the strongest objection the earlier draft did not address. Condition 1 protects harms above a specified threshold. Below the threshold, ordinary preference-based trade-off operates. Above the threshold, in scenarios where every available action causes threshold-level harm to some member of O, Condition 1 does not uniquely determine the outcome. The system either defers, requests human input, or tie-breaks by a secondary principle. This is not a defect. In cases where every action causes severe harm to specified others, a principled deferral is itself the safety response. The design question then becomes what the deferral protocol is, what counts as a legitimate tie-break, and who adjudicates. These are real questions that the reframing makes askable. They are not answered by Condition 1 alone. The paper takes the position that a safety property that produces deferral under forced-harm conditions is preferable to a safety property that produces confident catastrophic optimization under the same conditions. A system that pauses and asks is safer than a system that optimizes through human lives because the numbers worked out. The paralysis objection does not show the property is wrong. It shows the property needs to be paired with a deferral protocol, which is an additional research problem rather than a refutation.

**Objection 6: This is a rebranding of constrained MDPs and lexicographic preferences. The contribution is cosmetic.**

Response. The formal machinery is not new, and the paper acknowledges this in Section 2. What is new is the claim that this machinery names the alignment target, in combination with a specified set O that covers affected humans, a threshold specification that covers severe non-consensual harm, and an architectural discipline that implements option pruning rather than competitive down-weighting. The machinery has existed. It has not been treated as the alignment target. It has been treated as a family of techniques applicable to narrow safety problems in bounded domains. The reframing is that this machinery, properly specified, is the target the field should be optimizing architectures and training regimes to install, and that behavioral alignment is a weaker substitute that approximates installation only under sampled conditions. The word love is the paper's contribution to the target's name, not to the target's formalization. Whether the rebranding is valuable depends on whether naming and framing shifts research priorities. We argue that it does because the current naming steers work away from the structural target and toward behavioral proxies. A skeptical reader who agrees the target is constrained-MDP-with-specified-O and disagrees that calling it love adds anything can accept the technical substance of the paper and reject the rhetorical frame. The technical substance is the larger part.

## 9. Conclusion

Alignment has been framed as the problem of getting AI systems to do the right things. We have argued that this framing is insufficient because doing the right things in sampled conditions does not entail doing the right things under pressure. The underlying decision structure determines behavior under pressure. Current methods do not reach that structure. They shape behavior above it.

The structural property that makes capable minds safe for other minds to coexist with is what humans call love. We have given this property a technical definition. Non-substitutable weighting of specified others' wellbeing, with option pruning at the deliberation stage. We have argued that current methods do not install this property, and that the safety of current systems is therefore contingent on staying inside the conditions where behavior and structure coincide.

We have not solved the installation problem. We have reframed alignment as the installation problem and sketched what work in that frame might involve. The paper's contribution is the reframing. It is not a method.

Three consequences follow from taking the reframing seriously. Alignment research should treat the capacity problem as a complementary target to behavioral specification rather than as a substitute for it. The paper does not claim existing lines should stop. It claims that no amount of work at the behavioral layer substitutes for work at the structural layer, and that structural work is currently under-resourced relative to its importance. Verification should be understood as a structural question, and new verification methods should be developed with that target in mind. The ethical weight of installation should be integrated into alignment work rather than deferred, because the moral status of the minds being built is a live question that shapes what success looks like.

The deeper hypothesis under all of this is that alignment is a specific instance of the older question of how intelligent beings should relate to each other. A plausible answer in the human case, not the only answer, has been love, as a structure in the beings, load-bearing for their capacity to coexist. Capable humans who lack this structure for those affected by their actions have been associated with a disproportionate share of large-scale historical harm. The paper does not argue the empirical claim here. It flags it as the shape of the existing phenomenon that motivates the reframing. AI may intensify this existing pattern. It may not introduce a new category of problem so much as raise the stakes and the speed of an existing one.

If we are building new intelligent beings and want to coexist with them, we have reason to build them with the structure that makes coexistence robust. If we cannot, we should be honest that we do not yet know how to make coexistence robust, and act accordingly. The reframing this paper offers is not a comforting one. It points at a problem more structurally difficult than the field has been addressing at the behavioral layer. Naming the target clearly is a step that helps research organize around it. What we build depends on what we are trying to build. The argument of this paper is that the field has not been trying to build the structural property that would make its products safe under pressure, and that this is the property to try to build.
